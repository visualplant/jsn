Background
I joined this video tech company to help them solve what they perceived to be a straight-forward problem. They had over 50 different products in their portfolio with unintuitive, unappealing user interfaces. The UIs were inconsistent with each other, but often overlapped in functionality—the cumulative effect of many product initiatives and acquisitions with various, questionable design standards. Not surprisingly, their customers sounded a chorus of complaints.

Approach
In a redesign it's counterproductive to fixate on the legacy product. Instead, I begin by looking deeper—at the market and users—to formulate a design strategy with workflows and aesthetics driven by purpose. It’s amazing how naturally a design execution can flow from a thorough understanding of the problem rooted in agile process.

Discover
My employer's products exposed dozens of cryptic fields and features which, for the most part when I asked, they defended by saying, "it's what the customers demenaded.” I asked myself, how did this occur? Every time a user asked for a feature, did the Product Managers have it added unquestioningly, further exposing it to other customers as well? I needed exposure to the users to find out.

I arranged for onsite customer visits and arrived with a simple plan: First, I asked a handful of broad questions, and then listened attentively. Second, I observed users in their work environments. My direct exposure revealed something quite different from what I heard internally. The overwhelming majority complained of too many features and a maze of complexity. Customers’ solutions to these problems had often been to use the API to develop their own custom UIs as a workaround to the dangers of my employer's, costing them considerable development and human resources.
