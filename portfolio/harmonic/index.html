Background
I joined this video tech company to help them transition their legacy hardware products to modern, cloud-native software and, in the process, solve what they perceived to be a straight-forward problem. They had over 50 different product UIs in their portfolio with non-intuitive workflows and unappealing aesthetics. The UIs were inconsistent with each other, bound by no apparent ecosystem and often overlapped in functionality—the cumulative effect of many product initiatives and acquisitions with various, questionable design standards.
Before—50+ products with no ecosystem. Non-intuitive, inconsistent UIs competing with each other
 
Approach
In a redesign it's counterproductive to fixate on the legacy product. Instead, I begin by looking deeper—at the market and users—to formulate a design strategy with workflows and aesthetics driven by purpose. A great design flows from a thorough understanding of the problem, a grounded strategy and an execution rooted in an agile design process.

Discover
My employer's legacy products exposed dozens of cryptic fields and features which they defended by saying, "it's what the customers demanded.‚Äù For the most part, every time a user requested a feature, the Product Managers had it unquestioningly added, further exposing it to thousands of other users. This approach inevitably resulted in 'death by a thousand cuts' and code bloat, hampering agility. The dynamic between my employer and their customers needed to change before the product design could improve.

My cyclical discovery process involved a combination of internal interviews, competitor analysis, customer visits and product usage data. For onsite visits, I came with a simple plan: First, I asked a handful of broad questions, and listened attentively. Second, I passively observed users in their work environments. My direct exposure revealed something quite different from what I heard internally. Users were paralyzed with a maze of complexity in the products. And the disorganized UI was scaring them away to the competition.

Define
With a plethora of useful data discovered and documented, I collaborated with my managers to interpret it and formulate a product plan. Naturally, this involved a lot of trial-and-error, so we quickly presented ideas on the white-board and captured them to a photo stream for subsequent review and discussion. Then, we weeded out the best from the rest, and reached consensus to proceed.

Fig 7: White-boarding
 
I solidified ideas further giving them structure in the form of semi-functional wireframes and umbrella schematics which defined platform structure, taxonomy and labeling. This would be the springboard for an interaction framework, including clear definitions for scaffolding and semantic aspects of user experience.
